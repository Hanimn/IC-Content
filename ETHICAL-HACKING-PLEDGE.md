# The Ethical Hacking Pledge

## Read This Before You Start

The skills in this series are real. Cybersecurity professionals use these exact techniques every day to find and fix vulnerabilities in websites and apps. That's a good thing — it makes the internet safer for everyone.

But these same skills can cause serious harm if misused. Before you learn them, you need to understand the rules.

---

## The One Rule That Matters

> **Only test systems you own, or systems where you have explicit written permission to test.**

That's it. One rule. Everything else follows from it.

---

## Why This Rule Exists

Computer fraud laws are real in almost every country. In the United States, the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) makes it a federal crime to access a computer system without authorization — even if you didn't break anything, even if you were just curious, even if the vulnerability was obvious.

"I was just looking" is not a defense. "I only found the flag, I didn't do anything bad" is not a defense. The law looks at *access*, not intent.

This isn't meant to scare you. It's meant to be honest with you, because you're old enough to understand it.

---

## What Makes Something Legal to Test

You have permission to test when:

1. **It's your own system.** You set it up, you own the account, you control it.
2. **You're in a CTF competition.** The organizers built the challenges specifically for you to attack. That's the whole point. Their rules page is your permission slip.
3. **A teacher or instructor set up a practice environment.** This series is an example — every challenge file here was built for you to break.
4. **You have written permission from the owner.** Professional penetration testers always have a signed contract before they start.

If none of those apply: **stop and ask before you touch anything.**

---

## What To Do If You Find a Real Vulnerability

Sometimes, while practicing on legitimate sites (not CTFs), you might notice something that looks like a real security flaw. Here's what to do:

1. **Do not exploit it.** Don't go further than confirming it exists.
2. **Tell a trusted adult** — a teacher, parent, or school IT person.
3. **Look up the site's responsible disclosure policy.** Many companies have a page called "Security" or "Report a Vulnerability" that explains how to report bugs. Reporting a vulnerability you found by accident, responsibly, is legal and often appreciated.

What you should *not* do: post about it publicly, show friends, or poke at it further out of curiosity. That path leads to real legal trouble, even if your intentions were good.

## The Pledge

By starting this series, you agree to the following:

> I will only use the skills I learn here on systems I have permission to test.
> I will not access, modify, or damage systems that don't belong to me.
> If I find a real vulnerability by accident, I will report it responsibly and not exploit it.
